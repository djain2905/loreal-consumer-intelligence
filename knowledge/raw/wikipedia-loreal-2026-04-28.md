L'Oréal S.A.

**L'Oréal S.A.** (French:[\[lɔʁe.al\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/French "Help:IPA/French")) is a French multinational personal care corporation registered in [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris")[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-:3-2) and headquartered in [Clichy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clichy,_Hauts-de-Seine "Clichy, Hauts-de-Seine"), Hauts-de-Seine.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-3) As of 2024, it is the world's largest cosmetics company.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-4)[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-5) As of the early 2020s, L'Oréal owned 36 brands and 497 patents.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-6)

## History

### Founding

In the early 20th century, [Eugène Paul Louis Schueller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Schueller "Eugène Schueller") (1881–1957), a French chemist, developed a hair dye formula called _l'Auréale_.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-7) Schueller formulated and manufactured his own products, which he sold to Parisian hairdressers.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-:5-8)[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-:6-9) On 31 July 1909, Schueller registered his company,[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-:4-10) the Société Française de Teintures Inoffensives pour Cheveux (Safe Hair Dye Company of France), which eventually became L'Oréal. In 1920, the company employed three chemists; the number of employees continued to grow, with 100 by 1950, and 1,000 by 1984; in 2021, there was an estimated total of 85,252 worldwide.[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-11)

After launching in the hair-colour business, L'Oréal branched out into other cleansing and beauty products. As of 2020, L'Oréal marketed in all sectors of the beauty business: hair colour, hair styling, body and skincare, cleansers, makeup, and fragrance.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-:5-8)[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-:6-9)

### Recent history and acquisitions

L'Oréal purchased Synthélabo, a pharmaceutical company, in 1973. Synthélabo merged with [Sanofi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanofi "Sanofi") in 1999 to become [Sanofi-Synthélabo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanofi-Synth%C3%A9labo "Sanofi-Synthélabo"). Sanofi-Synthélabo merged with [Aventis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aventis "Aventis") in 2004 to become [Sanofi-Aventis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanofi-Aventis "Sanofi-Aventis").[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-12)

Nestlé has owned a stake in L'Oréal since 1974, when it bought into the company at the request of [Liliane Bettencourt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liliane_Bettencourt "Liliane Bettencourt"), the daughter of the founder of L'Oréal.[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-13)

In 1984, L'Oréal acquired part of [Helena Rubinstein Incorporated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helena_Rubinstein_Incorporated "Helena Rubinstein Incorporated") from Albi International. The portions acquired were the Japanese and Latin American divisions of the company, along with overseeing the operations and the markets for those divisions.[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-WEILJ-14) The joint venture partnership between L'Oréal and Albi International lasted until 1988, when L'Oréal brought the remainder of the company and has owned the entire company since then.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-15)

From 1988 to 1989, L'Oréal controlled the film company [Paravision International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paravision_International "Paravision International"), whose properties included the [Filmation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filmation "Filmation") and [De Laurentiis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Laurentiis_Entertainment_Group "De Laurentiis Entertainment Group") libraries. [StudioCanal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/StudioCanal "StudioCanal") acquired the Paravision properties in 1994.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-16)

On 17 March 2006, L'Oréal purchased cosmetics company [The Body Shop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Body_Shop "The Body Shop") for £562 million.[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-17) In May 2008, L'Oréal acquired YSL Beauté for $1.8 billion.[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-18)

In 2014, L'Oréal finalized the acquisition of major Chinese beauty brand Magic Holdings for $840 million.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-19) In February 2014, L'Oréal agreed to buy back 8% of its shares for €3.4bn from [Nestlé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9 "Nestlé"). In February 2014, [Shiseido](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiseido "Shiseido") agreed to sell its Carita and Decléor brands to L'Oréal for €227.5 million (US$312.93 million (2014)).[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Shiseido_Sells_Carita,_Decl%C3%A9or_to_L'Or%C3%A9al-20) In June 2014, L'Oréal agreed to acquire [NYX Cosmetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NYX_Cosmetics "NYX Cosmetics") for an undisclosed price, bolstering its makeup offerings in [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America").[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-21) Also that year, L'Oréal announced it had agreed to purchase Brazilian hair care company Niely Cosmeticos Group for an undisclosed amount.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-22) In September 2014, L'Oréal acquired the brand Carol's Daughter.[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-23) In July 2016, L'Oréal agreed to acquire IT Cosmetics for $1.2 billion.[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-24)

In 2017, Bettencourt died and left the business to her daughter, [Françoise Bettencourt Meyers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7oise_Bettencourt_Meyers "Françoise Bettencourt Meyers").[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-:5-8)

In 2018, L'Oréal acquired the beauty technology company ModiFace.[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-25) In May 2018, L'Oréal announced a new beauty and fragrance partnership with [Valentino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valentino_(fashion_house) "Valentino (fashion house)").[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-26) In December 2020, L'Oréal announced that it would acquire Takami Co, a Japanese skincare company.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-27)

In 2021, L'Oréal announced the acquisition of the vegan skincare brand Youth to the People.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-28)[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-29)

In April 2023, L'Oréal purchased Australian luxury cosmetics brand [Aesop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aesop_(brand) "Aesop (brand)") for US$2.53 billion.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-30)[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-31)[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-32) In December 2023, L'Oréal announced its acquisition of the Danish research company Lactobio.[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-33)[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-34)

In 2024, L’Oréal announced its acquisition of Gjosa, a Swiss water conservation [startup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Startup_company "Startup company").[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-35)[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-36) In February 2024, L’Oréal announced its signing of a long-term global licensing agreement for the creation, development and distribution of luxury beauty products by the Italian brand [Miu Miu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miu_Miu "Miu Miu").[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-37)[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-38)

In August 2024, L'Oréal opened a research and innovation center in India.[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-39)[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-40)[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-41) In December 2024, L'Oréal expanded its portfolio by acquiring a stake in a leading Korean cosmetics company Gowoonsesang Cosmetics and its brand Dr.G from Swiss retailer [Migros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migros "Migros").[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-42)[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-43) In March 2025, L’Oréal sold hair care brand Carol's Daughter to an unnamed entrepreneur.[\[44\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-44)

In June 2025, L'Oreal signed an agreement to acquire haircare brand Color Wow.[\[45\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-45)

In October 2025, L'Oreal announced a deal to buy [Kering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kering "Kering")'s beauty business for approximately €4 billion. The deal includes cologne brand House of Creed and exclusive rights to develop fragrances and beauty products for Kering labels—including Gucci, Bottega Veneta and Balenciaga—for 50 years.[\[46\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-46)

## Corporate affairs

### Head office

L'Oréal Group has its head office in the Centre [Eugène Schueller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Schueller "Eugène Schueller") in [Clichy, Hauts-de-Seine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clichy,_Hauts-de-Seine "Clichy, Hauts-de-Seine"), close to [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris").[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-47) The building, constructed in the 1970s from brick and steel, replaced the former Monsavon factory, and employees moved into the facility in 1978. 1,400 employees work in the building.[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-48) In 2005, Nils Klawitter of _[Der Spiegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Der_Spiegel "Der Spiegel")_ said "the building, with its brown glazed façade of windows, is every bit as ugly as its neighbourhood." Klawitter added that the facility "gives the impression of a high-security zone" due to the [CCTV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-circuit_television "Closed-circuit television") cameras and security equipment.[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-49)

International units include:

- L'Oréal USA, changed from **Cosmair** in 2000[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-50) \- has its headquarters in [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City"), and is responsible for operations in the [Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas").[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-L'Or%C3%A9al_USA-51)
- L'Oréal Canada Incorporated - Canadian operations, based in [Montreal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montreal "Montreal")
- L'Oréal Australia - head office is in [Melbourne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melbourne "Melbourne")
- L'Oréal Nordic - head office is in [Copenhagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copenhagen "Copenhagen"), Denmark
- L'ORÉAL Deutschland GmbH - legal seat is in [Karlsruhe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karlsruhe "Karlsruhe"), head office is in [Düsseldorf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf "Düsseldorf")[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-52)

### Research and development facilities

L'Oréal has 21 worldwide research and development centers: three global centers in France: [Aulnay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aulnay-sous-Bois "Aulnay-sous-Bois"), [Chevilly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chevilly-Larue "Chevilly-Larue") and Saint-Ouen. Six regional poles include one in the United States: [Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clark,_New_Jersey "Clark, New Jersey"), [New Jersey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Jersey "New Jersey"); one in [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan"): [Kawasaki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kawasaki,_Kanagawa "Kawasaki, Kanagawa"), [Kanagawa Prefecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanagawa_Prefecture "Kanagawa Prefecture"); in 2005, one was established in [Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai "Shanghai"), [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), another in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"): [Mumbai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mumbai "Mumbai"), one other regional pole in [Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil "Brazil"): [Rio de Janeiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro "Rio de Janeiro") and, lastly, another was established in [South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa "South Africa"): [Johannesburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannesburg "Johannesburg").[\[53\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-53)

### Joint ventures and minority interests

L'Oréal holds 10.41% of the shares of [Sanofi-Aventis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanofi-Aventis "Sanofi-Aventis"), the world's number three and Europe's number one pharmaceutical company. The **Laboratoires Innéov** is a joint venture in nutritional cosmetics between L'Oréal and [Nestlé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9 "Nestlé"); they draw on Nestlé's knowledge in the fields of nutrition and food safety.

## Marketing

In [Kosovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosovo "Kosovo"), during the growth years of the mail-order business, L'Oréal and [3 Suisses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_Suisses "3 Suisses") founded Le Club des Créateurs de Beauté for mail-order sales of cosmetic products, with brands including Agnès b., Commence and Professeur Christine Poelman among others. In March 2008, L'Oréal acquired three Suisse's stake, taking sole control of the company.[\[63\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-63) In November 2013, L'Oréal announced that Le Club des Créateurs de Beauté would cease activity in the first half of 2014.[\[64\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-64)

Since 1997, L'Oréal has been an official partner of [The Cannes Film Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Cannes_Film_Festival "The Cannes Film Festival").[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-65) In the years of L'Oréal sponsorship, many L'Oréal beauty ambassadors walked the red carpet of the [Cannes Film Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival "Cannes Film Festival"). In 2017, L'Oréal beauty ambassadors, including [Julianne Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julianne_Moore "Julianne Moore"), [Susan Sarandon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Susan_Sarandon "Susan Sarandon"), [Andie MacDowell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andie_MacDowell "Andie MacDowell"), and [Eva Longoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eva_Longoria "Eva Longoria"), were responsible for the film selection for the outdoor cinema during the Cannes Film Festival.[\[66\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-66)

L'Oréal's advertising slogan, "Because I'm worth it", was created by a 23-year-old English art director and introduced in 1973 by the model and actress Joanne Dusseau.[\[67\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-67) In the mid-2000s, this was replaced by "Because you're worth it". In late-2009, the slogan was changed again to "Because we're worth it".[\[68\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-68)[\[69\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-69)

In 2010, significant growth occurred in Indonesia with a 61 percent increase of unit sales or 28 percent of net sales.[\[71\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-71)

In 2020, chief digital officer Lubomira Rochet reported in a video conference of the growing importance of e-commerce for the company, remarking that e-commerce makes 24% of its turnover in the third quarter of the year. ## Controversies

### Involvement in fascism by L'Oréal key figures

L'Oréal has conceded that its founder, [Eugène Schueller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Schueller "Eugène Schueller"), was a [Nazi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi "Nazi") sympathizer and antisemitic [fascist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascist "Fascist").[\[113\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-113)[\[114\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Business_Notes_Scandal_L'Oreal's-114) He was also a member of [La Cagoule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Cagoule "La Cagoule"), a violent, pro-fascist and [anti-communist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-communism "Anti-communism") organisation. During the [occupation of France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vichy_France "Vichy France") in [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Its founder formed [Mouvement Social Révolutionnaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouvement_Social_R%C3%A9volutionnaire "Mouvement Social Révolutionnaire") (MSR, Social Revolutionary Movement), a political party which collaborated with the Vichy government.[\[115\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-115) Schueller provided financial support for La Cagoule, and allowed some of the group's meetings to be held at L'Oréal headquarters. La Cagoule perpetrated crimes including firearms transportation, assassinating a former minister, and firebombing six synagogues.[\[116\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Andr%C3%A9_Bettencourt-116)[\[117\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Pascal-117) L'Oréal hired several members of the group as executives after World War II, such as [Jacques Corrèze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Corr%C3%A8ze "Jacques Corrèze"), who served as CEO of the United States operation.[\[118\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-118)[\[119\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-119)

Another controversy arose after [Jean Frydman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Frydman "Jean Frydman"), a shareholder and board member of Paravision, a film subsidiary of L'Oréal, was fired. He claims that he was let go because L'Oréal wanted to avoid an Arab boycott of businesses associated with Jews. In turn, Frydman decided to expose the past of L'Oréal executives. [André Bettencourt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Bettencourt "André Bettencourt") who married Schueller's daughter, [Liliane Bettencourt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liliane_Bettencourt "Liliane Bettencourt"), and became deputy chairman for L'Oréal, wrote 60 articles for La Terre Française, an antisemitic Nazi propaganda sheet. André has admitted ownership of the [propaganda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propaganda "Propaganda") but claimed he was poisoned by the Vichy regime and said, "I have repeatedly expressed my regrets concerning them in public and will always beg the Jewish community to forgive me for them."[\[116\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Andr%C3%A9_Bettencourt-116) André Bettencourt also sheltered Schueller and several collaborators from the [French Resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Resistance "French Resistance") after Liberation.[\[117\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Pascal-117) It was also revealed that Eugène Schueller hired [Jacques Correze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Correze "Jacques Correze"), who was the honorary head of L'Oréal's U.S. affiliate, Cosmair, and was involved with La Cagoule.[\[114\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Business_Notes_Scandal_L'Oreal's-114)

Further controversy arose when it was revealed that L'Oréal had its German headquarters for over 30 years, before being sold in 1991, on land confiscated from a Jewish family during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"). The Jewish family has been battling for restitution from the company for three generations, the latest of which is Edith Rosenfelder, a [Holocaust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Holocaust "The Holocaust") survivor. Fritz Rosenfelder was forced to sell the house to a Nazi official, of which the family never received the proceeds of the sale. Instead, the family was deported. The Allies passed Jewish restitution legislation which states that transactions with Nazis, even if appearing to be with the owner's consent, can be considered invalid. As the land was sold to an offshoot of L'Oréal, which was later bought out in 1961 by L'Oréal, the company claims that it is not responsible for anything that happened before then. The basis for Rosenfelder's argument is that since the original sale was illegal, all subsequent sales are equally unlawful. There was restitution paid in 1951 to the [Jewish Restitution Successor Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_Restitution_Successor_Organization "Jewish Restitution Successor Organization"), though this was done without the family's consent and none of the money ever reached the family. A book by Monica Waitzfelder, daughter of Edith Rosenfelder, published in French as _L'Oréal a pris ma maison_ and in English as _L'Oréal stole my house!_, details how L'Oréal took over the Waitzfelder home in the [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") city of [Karlsruhe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karlsruhe "Karlsruhe") (after the Nazis had engineered the removal of the family) to make it its German headquarters.[\[120\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Gentleman-120) Monica Waitzfelder is quoted as saying, "All the other businesses which took Jewish property have since returned it, without any great debate. I don't understand why L'Oréal should be any different from the others." A case was brought before the Supreme Court in France, but the public prosecutor ruled that there could be no trial. As of 2007, she is bringing the case to the [European Court of Human Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights "European Court of Human Rights").[\[117\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Pascal-117)[\[120\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Gentleman-120)

### Garnier boycott

On 31 July 2014 during [Operation Protective Edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Protective_Edge "Operation Protective Edge") launched by the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) in the Gaza Strip, the Israel advocacy organisation [StandWithUs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/StandWithUs "StandWithUs") posted several Facebook photos of care packages, which they said were donated by Garnier Israel to female IDF soldiers.[\[121\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-121)[\[122\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-122)[\[123\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-123) This sparked several calls to boycott Garnier and L'Oréal worldwide.[\[124\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-124) Garnier disavowed the giveaway and stated on its [Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook") page, “Garnier USA is aware of recent activity in social media. It is very important to us that our fans know that Garnier worldwide promotes peace and harmony and has a strict policy of not getting involved in any conflict or political matter. Garnier was astonished to discover this in social media. After investigation, the hand-out of about 500 products appeared to be part of a one-time local retailer initiative. Garnier disapproves of this initiative managed strictly at local level and is very sorry to have offended some of its fans.” [\[125\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-125)

### Animal testing

L'Oréal began _[in vitro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_vitro "In vitro")_ tissue testing in 1979, and does not test any of its products or ingredients on animals anywhere in the world since 1989–14 years before it was required by regulation.[\[126\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-126) Controversy came from the fact that L'Oréal sells products in China, whose regulators conduct animal testing on cosmetics to be sold within its territory. Even though a ban on animal testing in China came into effect in January 2020, Chinese authorities still perform this practice for imported "ordinary" cosmetics.[\[127\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-127)[\[128\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-128)

Following L'Oréal's 2006 purchase of [The Body Shop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Body_Shop "The Body Shop"), which does not support animal testing, The Body Shop's founder [Anita Roddick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anita_Roddick "Anita Roddick") was forced to defend herself against allegations of "abandoning her principles" over L'Oréal's involvement on animal testing. Calls were made for shoppers to boycott The Body Shop.[\[129\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-129) L'Oréal sold The Body Shop to Brazilian group Natura Cosméticos in 2017.[\[130\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-130)

### Racist discrimination lawsuits

On 11 August 2005, the [Supreme Court of California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_California "Supreme Court of California") ruled that former L'Oréal sales manager Elyse Yanowitz had adequately pleaded a cause of action for retaliatory termination under the [California Fair Employment and Housing Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_Fair_Employment_and_Housing_Act "California Fair Employment and Housing Act"), and remanded the case for trial.[\[131\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-autogenerated1-131) The case arose out of a 1997 incident in which Jack Wiswall, then the general manager for designer fragrances, allegedly told Yanowitz to fire a dark-skinned sales associate despite the associate's good performance. When Yanowitz refused, Wiswall pointed to a "sexy" blonde-haired woman and said, "God damn it, get me one that looks like that." Wiswall retired as president of the luxury products division of L'Oréal USA at the end of 2006.[\[131\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-autogenerated1-131)

The company faced discrimination lawsuits in France related to the hiring of spokesmodels and [institutional racism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institutional_racism "Institutional racism"). In 2007, the Garnier division and an external employment agency were fined €30,000 for recruitment practices that intentionally excluded women of colour from promoting its hair wash, "Fructis Style".[\[132\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-132)

L'Oréal continues to sell skin whitening products, which have been criticised as "capitalising on women's insecurities due to [colourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colourism "Colourism")." They advertise these controversial products, which have been criticised for promoting a colonial attitude as well as having safety concerns,[\[133\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-133) on its website by claiming; "Achieve clear, translucent and radiant skin. Our skin whitening products work to fade dark spots and brighten skin to give you the fair, flawless complexion you desire."[\[134\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-134)

### False advertising

In May 2007, L'Oréal was one of several cosmetic manufacturers (along with Clinique, Estee Lauder, Payot, Lancôme)[\[135\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-135) ordered by the [Therapeutic Goods Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Therapeutic_Goods_Administration "Therapeutic Goods Administration") in Australia to withdraw advertising regarding the wrinkle removal capabilities of its products.[\[136\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-136)

In the UK, L'Oréal has faced criticism from [OFCOM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OFCOM "OFCOM") regarding the truth of its advertising and marketing campaigns concerning the product performance of one of its mascara brands. In July 2007, the British [Advertising Standards Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advertising_Standards_Authority_(United_Kingdom) "Advertising Standards Authority (United Kingdom)") attacked L'Oréal for a television advert on its "Telescopic" [mascara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mascara "Mascara"), featuring [Penélope Cruz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pen%C3%A9lope_Cruz "Penélope Cruz"), stating, "it will make your eyelashes 60% longer." In fact, it only made the lashes look 60% bigger, by separating and thickening at the roots and by thickening the tips of the lashes. They also failed to state that the model was wearing [false eyelashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eyelash_extensions "Eyelash extensions").[\[137\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-137)

In July 2011, the British Advertising Standards Authority took action against L'Oréal, banning two airbrushed [Lancôme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanc%C3%B4me "Lancôme") advertisements in the UK featuring actress [Julia Roberts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julia_Roberts "Julia Roberts") and supermodel [Christy Turlington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christy_Turlington "Christy Turlington"). The agency issued the ban after British politician [Jo Swinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jo_Swinson "Jo Swinson") argued that the two ads misrepresented reality and added to the self-image problem amongst females in the UK. L'Oréal acknowledged that the photos had been airbrushed but argued that the two cosmetic products could actually produce the results depicted in the ads and that the results of the products had been scientifically proven.[\[138\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-138)

In June 2014, the company reached an agreement with the U.S. [Federal Trade Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission "Federal Trade Commission") not to make claims about its anti-aging products unless it had credible scientific evidence supporting the claims. The settlement followed an investigation by the commission into claims being made in relation to two products, which the commission described as "false and unsubstantiated".

L'Oréal has a team of 400 members of staff who post content to [Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook") every day, according to Marc Menesguen, the company's chief marketing officer.[\[139\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-139)

### Patent lawsuit

In July 2017, the [University of Massachusetts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts "University of Massachusetts") Medical filed a patent lawsuit against L'Oréal. In August 2017, Dennis Wyrzykowski and his company, Carmel Laboratories LLC, joined the lawsuit. The two parties claimed that L'Oréal's brands used UMass' patented technology for skin creams with the chemical [adenosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine "Adenosine").[\[140\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-140) In 2021, a judge found the patents invalid. In 2022, the [U.S. Appeals Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_courts_of_appeals "United States courts of appeals") reversed the decision, stating that UMass could continue to purse its lawsuit.[\[141\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-141)

### Corporate misconduct

L'Oréal was fined by [Autorité de la concurrence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autorit%C3%A9_de_la_concurrence "Autorité de la concurrence") in France in 2016 for price-fixing on personal hygiene products.[\[142\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-142)

### Munroe Bergdorf

Main article: [Munroe Bergdorf race row incident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munroe_Bergdorf_race_row_incident "Munroe Bergdorf race row incident")

In August 2017, L'Oréal dismissed [Munroe Bergdorf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munroe_Bergdorf "Munroe Bergdorf"), a mixed-race [transgender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transgender "Transgender") model, after she responded to the [Unite the Right rally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unite_the_Right_rally "Unite the Right rally") in [Charlottesville, Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia "Charlottesville, Virginia"), by stating in a Facebook post: "Honestly I don't have the energy to talk about the racial violence of white people any more. Yes ALL white people"; the post was also quoted as saying that "\[white people's\] existence, privilege and success as a race is built on the backs, blood and death of people of colour", "racism isn't learned, it's inherited and... passed down through privilege" and that "white people" ought to "begin to admit that their race is the most violent and oppressive force of nature on Earth".[\[143\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-143) Shortly after terminating Bergdorf, L'Oréal released a statement claiming its commitment to "\[support\] diversity and tolerance towards all people irrespective of their race, background, gender and religion" and had terminated its partnership with Bergdorf because her comments were "at odds with those values".[\[144\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-theguardianonthelorealcismrow-144)[\[145\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-nytimeslorealdropstransgendermodel-145)

### Child labour

In May 2024, a [BBC World Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_World_Service "BBC World Service") documentary titled "Perfume's Dark Secret" uncovered widespread child labor in Egypt's jasmine fields, an essential source for fragrances used by major beauty brands, including L'Oréal. The investigation exposed that children, some as young as five years old, were engaged in the physically demanding task of picking jasmine flowers. These children worked under grueling conditions, often beginning their shifts at 3 a.m. and working in high temperatures. The documentary highlighted the poverty these children faced, with some earning as little as $1 per day, far below a living wage.

The BBC's findings sparked global concern over labor practices in the perfume industry, particularly regarding the exploitation of children in rural areas of [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt"). The country's jasmine flowers are a key ingredient in high-end perfumes produced by companies like L'Oréal, leading to questions about the ethical sourcing of raw materials in the beauty and fragrance sector. Despite claims by L'Oréal and other luxury brands of strict monitoring systems, the documentary suggested that these children had been overlooked by current auditing processes and that insufficient measures were in place to protect workers from exploitation.[\[153\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-153)

### nkd lawsuit

L'Oréal has been in a three-year trademark dispute with a small British [hair-removal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hair_removal "Hair removal") aftercare company called nkd on the grounds that its name might cause "customer confusion" with the L'Oréal "Naked" line of eyeshadow palettes and other make-up products.[\[154\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Graun-154) The nkd name was created in 2009, one year before the launch of the "Naked" line in the UK. According to Rebecca Dowdeswell, the owner of nkd, "The two brand names are spelt and pronounced differently, so I’ve always been pronounced 'n-k-d', they’ve always been 'naked'."[\[154\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Graun-154) Dowdeswell's trademark for nkd expired in 2019, but because of the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic") she forgot to renew it until 2022, a failure for which she readily admits responsibility. To date nkd has spent over £30,000 on legal fees and has had to shut down one of its two salons in its struggle with the £170 billion company.[\[154\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L'Or%C3%A9al#cite_note-Graun-154)
